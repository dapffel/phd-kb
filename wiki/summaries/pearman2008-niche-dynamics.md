## Citation

Pearman, P.B., Guisan, A., Broennimann, O. & Randin, C.F. (2008). Niche dynamics in space and time. *Trends in Ecology and Evolution*, 23(3), 149–158. DOI: 10.1016/j.tree.2007.11.005

## TL;DR

This review examines the assumption of niche conservatism underlying SDM-based predictions of species distributions under climate change. Evidence for both niche stasis and niche shifts is mixed across timescales from decades to millions of years; the authors argue that combining SDMs, phylogenetic methods, and evolutionary models can help identify species for which niche stasis is a supportable assumption.

## Key Concepts

- [[niche-conservatism]] — the tendency of a species' niche to remain unchanged over time; used inconsistently in the literature and not universally supported by evidence
- [[ecological-niche]] — distinction between fundamental and realized niche is central; niche shifts can result from ecological processes (realized niche) or evolutionary processes (fundamental niche)
- [[species-distribution-models]] — SDMs assume neither fundamental nor realized niche changes when projecting distributions, an assumption that may be violated
- [[model-extrapolation]] — projecting SDMs in space and time rests on niche stasis assumptions that are not always met
- [[phylogenetic-niche-conservatism]] — phylogenetic methods can test for conservatism by comparing niche similarity with phylogenetic distance

## Methodology

This is a review paper synthesising evidence from three methodological traditions: (1) SDM-based approaches that detect niche shifts via hindcasting or comparing native vs. invaded ranges; (2) phylogenetic approaches using Mantel tests to correlate phylogenetic distance with niche similarity; and (3) evolutionary models (Brownian motion, Ornstein-Uhlenbeck) that model niche trait evolution along phylogenies.

## Key Findings

1. Niche conservatism — the tendency for related species to have similar niches, or for a species' niche to change little over time — is used inconsistently in the literature and should not be assumed uncritically.
2. SDMs can detect niche shifts by hindcasting (predicting past distributions from current-climate models) or by comparing native and invaded ranges of exotic species.
3. Some invasive species show rapid niche shifts in less than 100 years: fire ants (*Solenopsis invicta*) and spotted knapweed (*Centaurea maculosa*) shifted niches between native and invaded ranges, while Argentine ants (*Linepithema humile*) showed little evidence of shift.
4. Phylogenetic analyses yield mixed results: Cuban *Anolis* lizards show no phylogenetic signal in climatic niche; dendrobatid frogs show niche divergence during speciation; New World warblers show conservatism in recent speciation but shifts at deeper phylogenetic branches.
5. Brownian motion serves as a null model for random niche evolution; Ornstein-Uhlenbeck models incorporate directional and stabilizing selection. Losos et al. (2003) failed to reject Brownian motion for *Anolis* lizards, suggesting niche evolution was not constrained.
6. Detection of niche shifts depends on spatial, temporal, environmental, and phylogenetic scale. Broennimann et al. (2007) detected rapid niche change over 120 years when comparing European and North American ranges of spotted knapweed. Phylogenetic signal was more evident at larger environmental scope and broader phylogenetic trees.
7. Neither niche stasis nor rapid niche shift prevails universally across the evidence reviewed; the frequency of niche shifts shows no clear association with ecological conditions, morphological characteristics, or evolutionary history.

## Limitations

- The review does not provide a quantitative meta-analysis of niche shift frequency or magnitude across studies.
- SDMs fitted on field data capture only the realized niche; estimating the fundamental niche requires costly manipulative experiments, limiting the ability to distinguish ecological from evolutionary niche changes.
- The evidence base is taxonomically uneven, with most examples from plants, insects, reptiles, and birds, and fewer from marine organisms or microorganisms.

## Open Questions

- What life-history traits or evolutionary characteristics make a species more prone to niche shifts (e.g., polyploidy, dispersal capacity, genetic variation)?
- How do environmental conditions influence the rate of niche change?
- Can multi-scale studies combining phylogenetic trees, SDMs, and physiological experimentation reliably disentangle fundamental from realized niche changes?
- How should SDM practitioners decide, for a given species, whether niche stasis is a defensible assumption for climate-change projections?
