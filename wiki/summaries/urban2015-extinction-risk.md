## Citation

Urban, M.C. (2015). Accelerating extinction risk from climate change. *Science*, 348(6234), 571–573. DOI: 10.1126/science.aaa4984

## TL;DR

A meta-analysis of 131 published predictions finds that 7.9% of species are predicted to become extinct from climate change, with extinction risk accelerating with temperature — threatening up to one in six species under current policies (RCP 8.5). The highest risks are in South America, Australia, and New Zealand; dispersal assumptions and extinction debt thresholds are the largest sources of uncertainty after climate trajectory itself.

## Key Concepts

- [[species-distribution-models]] — the majority of the 131 studies used SDMs/climate envelope models to predict extinction risk; SDMs predicted 7% extinction risk, the lowest among modelling techniques
- [[climate-change-extinction]] — central topic; extinction risk accelerates non-linearly with global temperature rise
- [[model-extrapolation]] — most studies extrapolate current climate–distribution correlations to novel future conditions, omitting important biological mechanisms
- [[dispersal-capacity]] — assumptions about dispersal (none, species-specific, universal) substantially alter predicted extinction rates
- [[extinction-debt]] — the threshold at which habitat loss commits a species to extinction is the second most important factor explaining variation in risk predictions

## Methodology

Bayesian Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) random-effects meta-analysis of 131 published multispecies predictions of extinction risk from climate change. Each study was weighted by sample size. The analysis evaluated how extinction risk varied by future temperature, taxonomic group, geographic region, endemism, modelling technique, dispersal assumption, and extinction threshold. Credible intervals not overlapping zero and ΔDIC > 4 were used to assess statistical support.

## Key Findings

1. Overall, 7.9% of species are predicted to become extinct from climate change (95% CI: 6.2–9.8%); results were robust to model type, weighting scheme, and potential publication bias.
2. Extinction risk accelerates with temperature: 2.8% at current warming (0.8°C), 5.2% at the 2°C policy target, 8.5% at 3°C (RCP 6.0), and 15.7% at 4.3°C (RCP 8.5) — one in six species.
3. South America (23%) and Australia/New Zealand (14%) face the highest extinction risks; North America (5%) and Europe (6%) the lowest. High-risk regions face no-analog climates and harbour endemic species with small ranges.
4. Endemic species face 6% greater extinction risk than non-endemic species, and the rate of risk increase with temperature is steeper for endemics.
5. Extinction risks did not vary significantly by taxonomic group, contrary to expectations — trait variation at finer taxonomic scales may be more important.
6. Extinction debt thresholds were the second most important factor: decreasing the habitat loss threshold from 100% (no debt) to 80% increased risk from 5% to 15%.
7. Dispersal assumptions significantly affected predictions: universal dispersal yielded 6%, species-specific 10%, and no dispersal 12% extinction risk.
8. Modelling technique had only a marginal effect, though species-area relationships (22%) and expert opinion (18%) produced higher estimates than SDMs (7%) or mechanistic models (8%).
9. 60% of predictions centre on North America and Europe, suggesting an urgent need to refocus research toward less studied and more threatened regions (e.g., only 4 studies in Asia).

## Limitations

- The majority of underlying studies extrapolate current climate–distribution correlations to novel conditions and omit species interactions, evolution, landscape dispersal barriers, habitat degradation, and intraspecific trait variation.
- Depending on the mechanism, its inclusion can either increase or decrease predicted risks (e.g., evolution may reduce risk; anthropogenic barriers may increase it).
- Extinction risk estimates are likely smaller than the total number of species affected by climate change — non-threatened species may still experience large changes in abundance, distribution, and interactions.
- The meta-analysis inherits the limitations of the individual studies it synthesises, most of which use correlative approaches.

## Connections

- The reliance on correlative SDMs connects to critiques in [[araujo2006-sdm-challenges]] and [[santini2021-sdm-reliability]] about the limitations of projecting SDMs into future climates.
- The geographic pattern of highest risk in regions facing no-analog climates aligns with [[williams2007-novel-climates]] and [[fitzpatrick2009-non-analog-climate]], which identify novel/non-analog environments as zones where SDM predictions are least reliable.
- The finding that dispersal assumptions strongly influence predictions connects to [[elith2010-range-shifting]], which argues that data treatment and biological realism matter more than algorithm choice.
- The accelerating risk with temperature provides an empirical anchor for the conservation urgency argued in [[guisan2013-sdm-conservation]] and the extinction projections in [[thuiller2005-climate-change-plants]].
- The question of whether species can evolve fast enough to track climate change relates to niche dynamics discussed in [[pearman2008-niche-dynamics]].

## Open Questions

- How do species interactions, evolutionary adaptation, and landscape barriers jointly modulate extinction risk beyond what correlative SDMs capture?
- Are the high extinction risks predicted for South America and Australia/New Zealand robust to more mechanistic modelling approaches?
- How should extinction debt thresholds be estimated on a species-by-species basis given differences in generation time and population size?
- What is the true extinction risk in understudied regions (Asia, Africa, tropical oceans)?
