## Citation

Williams JW, Jackson ST (2007) Novel climates, no-analog communities, and ecological surprises. Frontiers in Ecology and the Environment, 5(9):475–482. DOI: 10.1890/070037

## TL;DR

No-analog communities — assemblages of extant species in combinations not found today — occurred frequently during the late-glacial period and are tightly linked to climates also lacking modern analogs. Using IPCC ensemble simulations, Williams and Jackson show that novel climates will arise by 2100 primarily in tropical and subtropical regions, increasing the risk of species reshuffling, novel ecosystems, and ecological surprises that current models are poorly equipped to predict.

## Key Concepts

- [[non-analog-climate]] — central topic: climatic conditions with no present-day counterpart, both in the past (high seasonality) and future (unprecedented warmth in the tropics)
- [[no-analog-communities]] — communities of extant species in combinations not currently observed; an ecological (not evolutionary) phenomenon driven by novel climate states
- [[ecological-niche]] — niche-based framework explains how novel climates open unrealised portions of fundamental niches, enabling new species co-occurrences
- [[species-distribution-models]] — bioclimatic niche models are challenged because they are parameterised from modern observations and cannot predict responses in unobserved climate space
- [[model-extrapolation]] — ecological forecasts into novel climates are inherently extrapolative and therefore less certain
- [[individualistic-species-response]] — species respond to climate change independently in timing, magnitude, and direction; communities do not migrate as intact units

## Methodology

The paper integrates paleoecological evidence with future climate projections. For the past, it synthesises fossil pollen data from lake and mire sediments across North America to map no-analog vegetation during the late-glacial period (17,000–12,000 years BP), using minimum-dissimilarity comparisons against modern pollen assemblages. For the future, it analyses an ensemble of IPCC AR4 global climate model simulations, calculating a four-variable dissimilarity index (summer temperature, winter temperature, summer precipitation, winter precipitation) between 1980–1999 and 2080–2099 climates at ~2.8-degree grid resolution. A climate is classified as "novel" if its dissimilarity to its closest 20th-century match exceeds a threshold calibrated to discriminate same-biome vs. different-biome grid-cell pairs. Two emission scenarios are analysed: A2 (850 ppm CO2 by 2100) and B1 (550 ppm stabilised).

## Key Findings

1. Late-glacial no-analog plant communities in North America (17,000–12,000 BP) were characterised by anomalous co-occurrences of now-allopatric taxa (e.g., Picea with Fraxinus) and are linked to climates with higher-than-present temperature seasonality.
2. No-analog communities in Alaska and eastern North America were broadly contemporaneous despite being separated by ice sheets, suggesting a common atmospheric driver (enhanced insolation and temperature seasonality).
3. Under the IPCC A2 scenario, novel climates by 2080–2099 are concentrated in tropical and subtropical regions — lowland Amazonia, southeastern US, African Sahara/Sahel, eastern Arabian Peninsula, southeast India/China, IndoPacific, and northern Australia — because the warmest areas are the first to exit the present climate envelope.
4. Constraining analog searches to within 500 km (an upper bound for unassisted plant migration by 2100) makes regionally novel climates globally pervasive under A2.
5. The no-analog problem for niche models arises specifically when (a) the observed climate envelope truncates a species' fundamental niche and (b) climate change opens up that unobserved niche region.
6. Mechanistic models (e.g., dynamic global vegetation models) are in principle better suited for novel climates but are in practice still partially parameterised from modern observations.
7. Future novel climates concentrate in regions of high ecological complexity and biodiversity, compounding the risk of ecological surprises from species interactions, land use, invasions, and CO2 effects.

## Limitations

- The dissimilarity index uses only four climate variables (seasonal temperature and precipitation); other ecologically important dimensions (e.g., extreme events, CO2, soil properties) are not captured.
- The grid resolution (~2.8 degrees) is too coarse to capture regional climate heterogeneity and microrefugia that may buffer species from novel conditions.
- The paleoecological case study is restricted to North America and plant pollen; generalisability to other continents and taxa is assumed but not demonstrated.
- The niche-conservatism assumption may not hold for rapidly evolving taxa, and the paper acknowledges adaptive responses are poorly understood.
- The 500-km migration radius is a rough heuristic; actual migration capacity varies enormously among taxa.

## Open Questions

- Can paleoecological records of no-analog communities be used to formally validate SDM predictions under novel climate conditions, or are past and future novel climates too different?
- How do CO2 fertilisation effects interact with novel thermal regimes to reshape species niches — does elevated CO2 expand or constrain the realised niche?
- What is the threshold of climate novelty beyond which community-level predictions become effectively impossible?
- How should conservation planning prioritise regions facing novel climates where model reliability is lowest?
