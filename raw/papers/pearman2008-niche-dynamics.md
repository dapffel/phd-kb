# Niche dynamics in space and time

**Authors:** Peter B. Pearman, Antoine Guisan, Olivier Broennimann, Christophe F. Randin
**Journal:** Trends in Ecology and Evolution (2008) 23(3):149–158
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.tree.2007.11.005
**Type:** Review

---

## Summary

Niche conservatism — the tendency of a species' niche to remain unchanged over time — is often assumed when applying SDMs to predict species distributions under climate change or biological invasions. This review examines evidence for both niche stasis and niche shifts across timescales from decades to millions of years, and argues that combining SDMs, phylogenetic methods, and evolutionary models can help identify species for which niche stasis is a supportable assumption.

## Key concepts

- **Niche conservatism**: tendency for related species to have similar niches, or for the niche of a species to change little over time. Used inconsistently in the literature.
- **Niche shift**: any change in position of fundamental or realized niche. Can result from ecological processes (changing biotic interactions → realized niche) or evolutionary processes (changing tolerances → fundamental niche).
- **Niche stasis**: absence of change. Neither niche stasis nor rapid niche shift prevails in current studies.

## The fundamental challenge

Using SDMs to project distributions in space and time assumes, potentially uncritically, that neither fundamental nor realized niche changes. Ideally, predictions should be based on the fundamental niche, but this can only be estimated via costly manipulative experiments. SDMs fitted on field data capture only the realized niche.

## SDMs and niche dynamics

SDMs can detect niche shifts by: (1) hindcasting — predicting past distributions from current-climate models; (2) comparing native and invaded ranges of exotic species. Examples: fire ants (Solenopsis invicta) and spotted knapweed (Centaurea maculosa) show niche shifts; Argentine ants (Linepithema humile) show little evidence of shift. Niche shifts have occurred in less than 100 years.

## Phylogenetics and niche dynamics

Combining phylogenetic distance matrices with niche similarity matrices (Mantel tests) can test for phylogenetic signal in niche traits. Examples: Cuban Anolis lizards show no phylogenetic signal in climatic niche; dendrobatid frogs show niche divergence during speciation; New World warblers show conservatism in recent speciation but shifts at deeper branches.

## Evolutionary models

Brownian motion serves as null model for random niche evolution. Family of models (Ornstein-Uhlenbeck) incorporates directional and stabilizing selection. Losos et al. (2003) failed to reject Brownian motion for Anolis lizards, suggesting niche evolution has not been constrained. These approaches can identify lineages prone to niche shifts vs. those with conservative evolutionary histories.

## Scale matters

Detection of niche shifts depends on spatial, temporal, environmental, and phylogenetic scale. Broennimann et al. (2007) detected rapid niche change over 120 years when comparing European and North American ranges of spotted knapweed. Cavender-Bares et al. (2006) found phylogenetic signal more evident with larger environmental scope and phylogenetic trees. Florida oaks show niche over-dispersion within clades but phylogenetic signal at broader scales.

## Evidence summary (Table 1)

Evidence for niche shifts and conservatism is mixed, with no clear association between niche shift frequency and any particular ecological conditions, morphological characteristics, or evolutionary history. Examples span character divergence, host shifts, invasive species, speciation events, and phylogenetic analyses across plants, insects, reptiles, mammals, birds, and marine organisms.

## What remains to be done

- Meta-analyses of correlates of niche dynamics
- Understanding how environmental conditions influence rates of niche change
- Identifying life-history traits associated with niche shift propensity (e.g., polyploidy, bimodal dispersal, genetic variation)
- Multi-scale studies combining phylogenetic trees and SDMs
- Combining phylogenetic methods with physiological experimentation to disentangle fundamental vs. realized niche changes
