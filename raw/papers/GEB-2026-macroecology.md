Abstract 
 
Background. Macroecology began, in practice, as a pattern-extraction science to assemble large 
datasets, find patterns and hypothesize mechanisms. Since the original conception of 
macroecology in 1989, the unit of value in the published record was a fitted model such as a 
body-size distribution or environmental drivers of species richness. 
 
Argument. Capable machine-learning tools have now commodified pattern extraction. What 
macroecology produced for three decades is increasingly generated with a computer and open 
datasets. The intellectual value of a paper has therefore shifted away from the analysis to 
deciding what data should be gathered, how data should be collected, and interrogating what a 
fitted model can and cannot causally support. This shift from pattern-extraction to 
design-and-inference is further enabled by AI tools, absorbing the tedious data compilation, 
collation, and description inherent to macroecological workflows.  
 
Counterargument and constraints. Hypothesis generation, sampling design, and inferring 
causes from data may also soon be automated. We identify the durable human contributions as 
the ones machines cannot make: deciding what is worth measuring, recognising what is missing 
from the data, and choosing among options no one has thought to put on a list. AI is returning 
time to macroecologists, and we argue there is an obligation to increasingly innovate our 
discipline and those tasks that are not yet automatable. 
 
Recommendations. We close by suggesting criteria for what the next decade's most impactful 
macroecology papers will look like, and how the field should train people to meet the 
opportunities of our time. The way we judge the merit of research should change from credit 
given to multiple                 studies oriented towards pattern-extraction            towards prizing 


slower, higher-effort, work where novel data and data-streams are combined with robust 
inference.   
 
 
 
1. Where macroecology began 
 
Brown and Maurer (1989) introduced the term macroecology in response to a reductionist turn in 
ecology that had lost sight of larger scales of space and time. Macroecology was founded on 
modest methods, including frequency distributions, bivariate plots, and continental species 
distribution datasets laboriously assembled from field guides and museum collections. But even 
at the outset, macroecology was ambitious in its goals to detect general patterns empirically, 
formulate mechanistic hypotheses to explain them, and test those hypotheses against the 
diversity of natural systems (Brown, 1999). Almost every criticism directed at the field since 
(Gaston & Blackburn, 1999) has been a variation on a single theme: patterns at large scales are 
easy to see and hard to explain, because the processes that generate them operate at scales that no 
single investigator can manipulate. 
 
Scale is therefore the foundational problem of macroecology. Indeed, many have argued there is 
no single correct scale at which macroecological questions should be asked, as extent and 
resolution must be chosen with reference to the question driving a study. Ideally the same 
question should be asked at several scales to see which factors emerge as important (Blackburn 
and Gaston 2002). For instance, an analysis of South American hummingbirds revealed that 
climate best predicts species richness at smaller spatial scales while topography dominates at 
larger ones (Rahbek and Graves 2001). Another example is the right-skewed body-size 
distribution of North American mammals at the continental scale, which flattens out at the level 
of local communities (Brown and Maurer 1989). In both cases, this scale-dependent pattern 
highlights where key mechanisms are at play. 
 
For three decades the discipline of macroecology has grown by drawing continental-scale 
questions from biogeography, the tools of inference from statistics, and fine-grained 
measurements at large extents from remote sensing. Most recently macroecology has benefited 
from machine-learning methods that can handle both data volumes and nonlinearities that 
classical methods cannot. Each of these borrowings has been celebrated as a gain, and each has 
raised the same uncomfortable question: what is the role of a macroecologist? In this perspective 
we argue that AI is shifting macroecology's focus from pattern-extraction to 
design-and-inference, and that the discipline's task now is to train its next generation for the 
work this shift has left in human hands. 
 
2. The shift: from pattern-extraction to design-and-inference 
 
Macroecology is undergoing a shift accelerated by the commodification of pattern extraction. For 
most of its history, the unit of value in a macroecology paper was a fitted model to a large 
dataset. These are pattern-extraction outputs. Producing such outputs required substantial labour 
such as assembling the data, cleaning the taxonomy, fitting the model, checking the diagnostics, 


drawing the figures. That labour, and the skill it required, was much of what the field rewarded 
as it documented and revealed patterns at scale. 
 
It is now 2026, and some of the labour is supported by machine-learning tools and increasingly 
capable AI assistants. Indeed, a graduate student in 2026 can download observations of species 
occurrences from a global database (e.g., the Global Biodiversity Information Facility, GBIF 
(https://www.gbif.org) run a deep species distribution model with a single command, and 
generate a global climate projection in an afternoon (Brun et al., 2024). A reviewer can re-fit the 
same model under three alternative parameterisations between meetings. A pre-built 
machine-learning pipeline can classify 42,000 benthic reef images overnight (Kennedy et al., 
2020). Once the computational pipeline is set, these analytic steps can be rapidly executed. But 
what is not presently automated is judgement about what to ask, what to measure, where biases 
lie, and what to conclude from modelling approaches that also come with inherent biases and 
assumptions.  
 
This is a shift from pattern-extraction to design-and-inference. By ‘design’ we mean the 
upstream decisions: which question is worth asking, which dataset needs to exist that does not 
yet exist, which protocol should be used across which sites, and which collaboration should be 
assembled to do it. By ‘inference’ we mean the downstream decisions: what does a fitted model 
allow one to claim, under which assumptions, and against which alternative hypotheses. These 
intellectual activities will presumably resist automation because they require commitments to 
objectives, values, and counterfactuals that are not contained in any existing dataset and that no 
algorithm can derive without being told what to optimize. 
 
Thus it is inevitable that some categories of macroecology papers will ultimately become 
obsolete. The descriptive paper that purports to fit a familiar model to an existing public dataset 
and reports a pattern can now be supported by AI. Such papers will continue to be written, and 
journals will continue to publish papers produced by a classic formula, but the scientific gain and 
innovative contribution of these papers will be reduced. Indeed, such papers are likely to 
perpetuate known biases in the field, including strong temperate biases (i.e., the Wallacean 
shortfall). When models built on Global North data are uncritically extrapolated to the rest of the 
world, they risk producing fundamentally flawed conclusions due to the ecological and 
taxonomic differences that these formulas fail to capture. Instead, the papers that will define 
macroecology a decade from now are those that collected data that did not exist, coordinated an 
experiment no single lab could run alone, or interrogated a model in a way that revealed 
something about its limits.  
 
3. What future AI will presumably bring 
 
Looking to the future, hypothesis generation is already being attempted by large language 
models (LLM) trained on the scientific literature, which can propose multiple plausible-sounding 
mechanisms for an observed pattern in seconds (Cohrs et al. 2025; Gougherty and Clipp 2024). 
Sampling design has mature solutions in principle: Bayesian optimal experimental design and 
active-learning algorithms can identify the next observation that would most reduce uncertainty 
about a target quantity, and these methods are already being applied to species distribution 
modelling and ecological monitoring. Causal discovery algorithms can learn directed acyclic 


graph structure from observational data without human-supplied priors, addressing inferential 
gaps in macroecology (Connolly et al. 2017). Reinforcement-learning agents can search 
experimental design spaces and propose protocols a human might not have considered. LLM 
agents can already write code, run analyses, and draft manuscripts. The reliability of these agents 
for bounded scientific tasks is still under active evaluation (Lu et al. 2024). 
 
A key question is thus whether the human macroecologist will be a transitional figure whose 
remaining tasks will be absorbed within a decade. Certainly optimal experimental designs do 
work; causal discovery algorithms can recover plausible causal networks from observational 
data; LLM agents can run analyses end-to-end. But even so, every one of these methods requires 
a well-specified set of objectives. Optimal experimental design tells you where to sample next 
given a target quantity to estimate but is unable to tell you which target quantity matters. The 
choice of target (species richness, functional diversity, phylogenetic diversity, evolutionary 
distinctness, ecosystem service value, resilience to a specified perturbation, the persistence of a 
culturally significant taxon) must be made by humans, and link to a question of what biodiversity 
components are being valued and for what reason. Different choices yield different optimal 
designs, and no algorithm can rank between choices without being told which to prefer. Another 
issue is that analyses and methods are fundamentally constrained by the input and training data. 
Causal discovery from observational data works by exploiting conditional independencies in 
existing datasets and is reliable only when the data span the relevant variation (Spirtes et al. 
2000; Runge et al. 2019). Macroecology's deepest problems are precisely those where data are 
missing: the tropical insects that have not been described, the deep-sea benthos that have not 
been sampled, the temporal baselines that were never collected, the historical reference points 
lost to land-use change.  
 
Analyses of data are further defined in advance by a human, such as active learning choices 
among candidate observations from a list. The decisions that matter most in macroecology are 
open-action decisions: which biome to invest a decade of fieldwork in, which institutional 
partner in which country to build a relationship with, which long-term monitoring programme to 
fund through the next political cycle, which protocol to standardise across a continental network. 
These decisions cannot be outlined in advance because their value depends on contingencies that 
are political, ecological, and social, but are not the object of study. 
 
AI is thus extraordinarily powerful within bounds, but macroecology is largely an open-world 
problem. The boundary is not an artefact of current technology that will move with the next 
model release, but rather a structural feature of what the field is trying to do. While aspects of 
macroecology will be automated, the human role is durable for the time being, especially if we 
anticipate and train our upcoming generation of scientists to become adept at the judgments 
required to move the field of macroecology forwards. Figure 1 summarises this distinction 
between bounded problems, where AI tools are effective, and the open-world decisions that 
macroecology actually depends on.

4. Designing the data streams that do not yet exist 
 
Macroecological data are inevitably uneven. The Wallacean and Linnean shortfalls are 
geographically and taxonomically structured in ways that reflect the history of Western biology 
as much as the biology itself (Wüest et al., 2020). Temperate vertebrates are well covered; 
tropical invertebrates, soil microbes, and the deep sea are not. Any global analysis that does not 
confront this unevenness is, at best, a global analysis of what we happen to have looked at. 
Machine-learning models trained on such data will reproduce and often amplify its biases, for the 
structural reason given in Section 3. 
 
This is a problem no algorithm can solve, because the missing data are missing. Someone has to 
go to the field and collect the data or deploy automated instruments to do so. The question for the 
next decade is how macroecologists should decide where to go and what to collect, and to 
develop the downstream processing pipelines that convert data into versions that can be analysed 
widely. Low-cost technologies now make it possible to collect high-quality data in places that 
were previously unaffordable. For example, unoccupied aerial systems (UAVs) and remotely 
operated vehicles (ROVs) can gather structured imagery in terrestrial and aquatic habitats at a 
fraction of the cost of crewed surveys, and often in habitats that crewed surveys cannot reach. 
Koontz et al. (2022) argue that integrating UAVs with the National Ecological Observatory 
Network can democratise the collection of fine-resolution ecological data, and that 
knowledge-sharing tools such as shared mental models can extend this democratisation to the 


global South where macroecological data are sparse (e.g., Bridges et al. 2023) . Bioacoustic 
monitoring extends the same logic to the soundscape: permanent acoustic nodes deployed by the 
National Oceanographic and Atmospheric Administration) have begun to fill temporal gaps in 
the migration patterns of soniferous species at scales no aerial survey could match (Haver et al., 
2018), and automated detection pipelines (Mouy et al., 2024) now make it practical to analyse 
the resulting archives. The asymmetry compounds: each machine-learning pipeline trained on 
today's biased data produces tomorrow's predictions, which then shape tomorrow's sampling 
priorities. Unless the underlying data streams are redesigned, AI entrenches the geography it 
inherited. 
 
The human task here is not to operate the sensor. The human task is to prioritise where the sensor 
should go based on all available information and logistical constraints, what it should measure, 
and how the resulting data will connect with the data already in existence. This is a design 
problem and reflects deep-rooted societal values and pragmatic constraints. Which taxa are 
under-represented in a way that most distorts our global understanding? Which biomes are most 
likely to cross ecological thresholds within the lifetime of the dataset being planned? Which 
communities of local researchers should lead the work, not merely participate in it? These 
decisions are the work of macroecologists, and they cannot be made by examining the existing 
data, because the whole point is that the existing data are a biased sample of the world. 
 
It is also important to recognize that the code used in many AI analyses is increasingly based on 
internal opaque methods, chosen by the AI models gnerated by very general prompts (“vibe 
coding”). Both defending the choices made by the AI models and reporting exactly what the AI 
model has done is becoming increasingly difficult. Common problems include code overload, 
large volumes of hard to review AI-generated code, loss of understanding of what the code 
actually does, and code that works but may not be stable over the longer-term. Macroecologist 
will not be judged based on their ability to produce code, but increasingly on their ability to 
produce transparent, replicable and robust AI pipelines.   
 
 
Likewise, macroecologists can begin to think of themselves as data-stream architects, not merely 
data-stream users. The generation that built global databases like GBIF (https://www.gbif.org), 
OBIS-SEAMAP (Halpin et al. 2009), MOVEBANK (Kays et al. 2022; Wikelski and Kays, 
2026), Reef Life Survey (Edgar and Stuart-Smith, 2014), GloBI and GIFT (Weigelt et al., 2019) 
made many key decisions, such as about what to standardise, what to leave open, how to credit 
contributors, how to handle licensing, that now structure the questions that can be asked. Those 
decisions were not technical, but instead editorial. The next generation of infrastructure will 
likely be built with machine-learning pipelines already embedded in it, and the editorial 
decisions will matter even more, especially for ensuring that uneven geographic and taxonomic 
coverage is addressed rather than silently amplified. 
 
5. Coordinating global experiments and recognising natural ones 
 
The most honest critique of correlative macroecology is that observational data can show 
patterns but cannot, on their own, identify causes. To address this shortfall, the field has built 
mechanistic and process-based models that explicitly encode candidate causal structures and 


interrogates those models carefully (Connolly et al. 2017). But mechanisms are causes, not 
components (e.g., animal thermotolerance, natural selection), and cannot be reduced to 
decomposable parts (McGill and Potochnik 2018). Macroecologists generally agree that 
observational data alone will not resolve causal questions. One potential alternative is 
experimentation at scale. Controlled study designs have long been considered impractical at 
macroecological extents, but two developments are changing that. The first is the increasing 
feasibility of hybrid datasets, in which experiments are nested within observational studies and 
used to inform mechanistic components of larger models. For instance, Benedetti-Cecchi et al. 
(2018) proposed exactly such a framework by deriving parameters experimentally at small scales 
and feeding them into joint species distribution models at large ones. This approach offers a 
realistic path from pattern to process.  
 
Another recognition is that nature sometimes runs the experiment for us. Natural experiments 
have been undervalued in macroecology and are now, we argue, one of the field's most exciting 
emerging directions. A striking example is in the North Pacific Ocean, where the abundance of 
odd- and even-year returns of pink salmon (Oncorhynchus gorbuscha) differ by more than 
2000%, producing a replicated perturbation across decades, sub-basins, and trophic levels. This 
life-history quirk provides a natural experiment revealing that the massive abundance of pink 
salmon is a key driver of interannual variation in the North Pacific food webs (Ruggerone et al. 
2023), with effects that reach as far as migrating seabirds in Australia (Springer et al., 2018). The 
COVID-19 pandemic provided a different kind of global natural experiment: lockdowns 
compared against pre-pandemic baselines quantified how humans both threaten and, when 
absent, release wildlife from pressure (e.g., Bates et al. 2021). And the increasing frequency of 
marine heatwaves is turning what were once rare climatological anomalies into a recurring 
experimental treatment whose effects on coral communities can be read off directly (e.g., Baum 
et al., 2023). For these examples, the connection to the open-action argument (Section 3) is 
direct. An AI agent could not have planned the pink salmon experiment because the experiment 
was not on any list of candidate actions: it was a contingent fact about a species' life history that 
became a research opportunity only after a human noticed it. A causal-discovery algorithm could 
not have planned the COVID-19 lockdown experiment because the perturbation was exogenous 
to the ecological system entirely. These are exactly the open-action, value-laden, 
contingency-dependent decisions that automated tools are not designed to make. Recognising 
how to take advantage of opportunities, building the baselines that make them interpretable, and 
assembling the international networks that can deploy a common protocol when a perturbation 
arrives requires the kinds of time and attention that machine-assisted analysis should now be 
freeing up. 
 
If the next decade of macroecology is going to be defined by process rather than pattern, the field 
should be investing in the infrastructure of coordinated experimentation such as protocol 
libraries, rapid-deployment funding mechanisms, shared sampling kits, and the human 
relationships that make transcontinental collaboration possible at the same time as it invests in 
computational skill and databases. This is because prioritization and coordination are needed for 
utilization and knowledge translation. A machine-learning pipeline that can classify a million 
coral images in an afternoon is only valuable if someone has arranged for those million images to 
be collected from the right reefs before, during, and after the next heatwave. 
 


6. Interrogating models 
 
The third area where humans are needed goes against the instinct to celebrate methodological 
novelty. Causal models in macroecology are powerful tools and include the mechanistic and 
process-based such as Maximum Entropy Theory of Ecology (Xiao et al., 2016) and its dynamic 
extension DynaMETE (Harte et al., 2021), the structural equation models that appear in global 
analyses (e.g., Edgar et al., 2017), or the deep neural networks whose hidden layers make 
straightforward interpretation difficult (Connolly et al. 2017). Yet these approaches are also 
fragile in specific ways that an uncritical user will miss. 
 
Three failure modes deserve to be taught explicitly (Connolly et al. 2017). First, two models 
sharing the same core assumptions can differ in auxiliary assumptions (i.e., the supporting 
choices made to fit the core model to data, such as the form of a dispersal kernel, the spatial 
grain, the prior on a parameter, or the handling of missing observations). Thus      models 
incorporating similar mechanisms can produce wildly different predictions, so that an apparent 
match between a model and the data may be tracking something the modeller did not intend. 
Second, the results of a mechanistic or process-based model are often so dependent on the 
assumptions used to generate them that the interesting scientific content can lie in those 
assumptions which ultimately determine the outputs. Thus, if the assumptions are not 
interrogated, the model has merely encoded the modeller's prior beliefs, arguably in more 
impressive notation. Third, the foundational mathematical and programming training required to 
interrogate complex models properly is lacking in many early career macroecologists, which 
means that the tools are being used faster than they are being understood.       and importantly the 
pitfalls of model application. 
 
A simple bibliographic exercise illustrates how far the field has to go. Using the Wiley advanced 
search tool on the archive of Global Ecology and Biogeography (conducted 26 January 2025), 
we searched for terms representative of null-hypothesis-testing statistics and terms representative 
of causal inference (Fig. 2). Over the life of the journal, the wildcard term "correlat*" returned 
1,611 articles and "regression" returned a comparable number, whereas "structural equation 
model*" and "path analysis" returned only 72 and 52 articles respectively. The term "acyclic" (a 
proxy for directed acyclic graphs (DAG), which form the backbone of modern causal statistics; 
McElreath, 2018) appeared in just two articles. Null search terms such as "and" and "the" 
returned ~2,900 articles each over the same period, confirming that the low counts for causal 
methods are not an artefact of coverage. In other words, the single most cited journal in the field, 
and the venue in which this perspective itself appears, contains roughly 30 times more articles 
using correlative statistics than articles using explicitly causal tools. The commitment to 
causation in review articles (e.g., Connolly et al., 2017; Beck et al., 2012) has not yet been 
matched by a methodological shift in the primary literature. If the transition from pattern to 
process is to be real rather than aspirational, this ratio is the one to watch over the coming 
decade.

Machine-learning models add their own version of each of these problems. They are data-hungry 
and sensitive to distributional shifts; a model trained on temperate-zone observations will 
confidently misclassify tropical ones.  Furthermore, we must admit a more fundamental flaw in 
our data streams, that they are filtered through human sensory biases. Most of the ecological 
sensors are designed to mimic human perception, capturing light and sound only within our 
limited visible and audible ranges. This anthropocentric bias may create fundamental data gaps 
that AI cannot resolve, as it can only optimize patterns within the filtered reality we provide. 
Such models are also often uninterpretable at the level of individual predictions, which makes it 
hard to know when the model is right for the wrong reason. Machine-learning models also have 
an insidious tendency to produce results that look exactly like science: tables, figures, confidence 
intervals, performance metrics. Interpretability, data-requirement, overfitting, and bias 
limitations of machine-learning methods in ecology should be required reading for anyone 
starting a project of this kind (Pichler and Hartig 2023). 
 
The human role is to ask whether the model's apparent success is coming from the features one 
hoped it was using, or from a spurious correlate that happens to track the response in the training 
data. It is to re-run the analysis under alternative assumptions and report the spread, not just the 
point estimate. It is to hold out not a random 20% of the data but a structured 20%, a biome, a 
continent, a decade, and see whether the model still produces similar estimates (Yates et al. 
2018). When the model fails these tests, authors thus need to report the failures in the paper 
rather than tucked away in a supplementary material. These activities all take time and therefore 
the single most valuable use of the time that machine-assisted workflows are now returning to 
macroecologists is, in our view,      the time spent interrogating models. For instance, a synthesis 
of mountain biodiversity shows what this looks like in practice: a correlative pattern is set 
alongside competing mechanistic explanations, and the predictions each would make under 
counterfactual conditions are stated explicitly, making the science falsifiable rather than merely 
descriptive (Rahbek et al 2019).  
 
These changes mean macroecologists will need to modify the way we judge the merit of 
research, making the incentives favor slower, higher-effort, work where novel data and 
data-streams are combined with extensive model interrogation and robust inference. Thus, we 
anticipate a strong shift from a focus on the volume of work (e.g., number of publications) to 
focus on research quality. We note that judging the quality of research has been an enduring 


challenge in science, with metrics such as impact factor and h-index having severe limitations. 
While AI-based methods may have some advantages also for assessing research quality, the 
challenge of identifying an excellent study will likely remain.  
 
 
 
7. What will make a macroecology paper impactful in the age of open data and open code 
 
If pattern extraction has been commodified, and the value of a macroecology paper has migrated 
to design and inference, then the criteria by which papers should be judged need to be made 
explicit. Below we offer seven concrete properties we believe the most valuable macroecology 
papers of the next decade will share.

Human perspectives and relationships can increase the value of every property in this table. 
Building the data streams that do not yet exist means working with the communities who live in 
those systems and know what has changed. Coordinating experiments at macroecological scales 
means assembling networks across institutions, countries, and knowledge systems. Human 
networks only hold together if the people in the network trust each other, which takes years of 
relationship-building that no funding cycle rewards adequately. Confronting geographic bias 
means going to the places that are under-sampled, but going there responsibly means working 
with local researchers and Indigenous knowledge holders who should be shaping what gets 
measured, not just granting access. Interrogating a model means asking whether its outputs make 
sense to someone who has watched that ecosystem for decades, not just to someone who can 
read a diagnostic plot. Ecological systems are lived in, managed, and known by people whose 
values, knowledge, and priorities should shape what macroecology chooses to ask. 
Co-production is how you discover that your novel hypothesis was already understood by a 
community managing that resource, or that the variable you omitted is the one an entire system 
depends on. The most important macroecology papers of the next decade might not be the ones 
that found a better algorithm but rather the ones that asked a better question. Better questions 
come from collaborations that change the question itself, not just the author list. 
 
8. Training the next generation 
 
If the field is shifting from pattern-extraction to design-and-inference, the way macroecologists 
are trained needs to shift with it. Five changes seem to us most urgent. First, mathematics and 
programming should be treated as core competencies of the discipline, not optional extras for 
methodologically inclined students; they are the only basis on which a student can interrogate a 
model rather than merely deploy one. Second, model interrogation with adversarial testing such 
as structured holdouts, sensitivity to assumptions, honest reporting of failures, should be taught 
alongside model fitting, and demonstrated as part of any causal-modelling assignment. Third, 
data-stream design should be introduced as a topic in its own right: what goes into building a 
database, what editorial choices shape the questions it can answer, and how the choices made 
twenty years ago, such as by the builders of GBIF or OBIS-SEAMAP, continue to shape what we 
can ask today. Fourth, the incentive structure facing early-career researchers should reward the 


slower, higher-effort work this shift demands. A graduate student who spends two years building 
an under-sampled data stream will publish later than one who re-analyses GBIF, and committees, 
funders, and journals should recognise the difference rather than penalise it. Fifth, we might 
prioritize the training of local young macroecologists within under-researched regions, such as 
the African continent and Pacific Islands. These researchers often have a strong understanding of 
their local ecosystems’ inner workings, independent of  the "prior conceptual pollution" of 
models originally built from temperate systems. By empowering those at the areas of the 
Wallacean shortfall, the field can move beyond the uncritical application to discover entirely new 
ecological patterns that currently lie beyond the boundaries of our existing knowledge.  
 
None of our suggestions reduces the importance of natural-history knowledge, field skills, or 
ecological intuition. If anything, it makes those things more valuable, because they are the 
human capacities that AI-driven tools cannot yet substitute for, and the capacities that determine 
whether a model's output makes biological sense. 
 
9. Conclusion 
 
Macroecology spent its first three decades doing something that has just become much easier. 
Pattern extraction is now sometimes a button-press, and a discipline that defines its value by 
pattern extraction will find itself with progressively less to offer. The opportunity is to relocate 
macroecology's labour: away from the steps AI now does well, and towards the processes it 
cannot perform.. The work that remains such as designing the data streams that do not yet exist, 
coordinating the experiments that nature alone cannot run, interrogating the models whose 
outputs are otherwise too easy to believe is harder, slower, and more contingent than the work 
that has been automated away. It is also where the science innovation will emerge. AI is not the 
threat to this transition but its enabling condition: by absorbing the laborious middle of the 
workflow, it returns to macroecologists the time and the obligation to do what only humans, for 
now, can do. Whether the field uses that time well will depend on whether it trains the next 
generation to recognise the difference between the pattern and the question, and to spend their 
hours, freed at last from the former, on the latter.
