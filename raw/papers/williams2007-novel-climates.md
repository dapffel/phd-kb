# Novel climates, no-analog communities, and ecological surprises

**Authors:** John W. Williams, Stephen T. Jackson
**Journal:** Frontiers in Ecology and the Environment (2007) 5(9):475–482
**DOI:** 10.1890/070037
**Type:** Review / Perspective

---

## Introduction

How do you study an ecosystem no ecologist has ever seen? This is a problem for both paleoecologists and global-change ecologists, who seek to understand ecological systems for time periods outside the realm of modern observations. One group looks to the past and the other to the future, but both use our understanding of extant ecosystems and processes as a common starting point for scientific inference. This is familiar to paleoecologists as the principle of uniformitarianism ("the present is the key to the past"), whereby understanding modern processes aids interpretation of fossil records. Similarly, global-change ecologists apply a forward-projected form of uniformitarianism, using models based on present-day ecological patterns and processes to forecast ecological responses to future change.

The further our explorations carry us from the present, the murkier our vision becomes. This is not just because fossil archives become sparser or because the chains of future contingency become increasingly long. Rather, the further we move from the present, the more it becomes an inadequate model for past and future system behavior. The current state of the Earth system is just one of many possible states, and both past and future system states may differ fundamentally from the present.

The paper focuses on "no-analog" plant communities, their relationship to climate, and the challenges they pose to predictive ecological models.

## Individualistic species shifts and no-analog communities: a conceptual framework

The formation and disappearance of no-analog communities are part of a more general phenomenon: the individualistic responses of species to environmental change. Ranges and abundances of terrestrial species shifted dramatically during the last deglaciation. Species varied widely in the timing, magnitude, and direction of these responses, and communities did not migrate as intact units (Gleason 1926; Davis 1981; FAUNMAP Working Group 1996; Jackson and Overpeck 2000).

Quaternary vegetation dynamics were driven by orbitally controlled glacial–interglacial climate cycles, as well as by sub-orbital (millennial) modes of climate variability. These environmental changes varied regionally and included changes in multiple variables (seasonal temperature, precipitation magnitude and timing, insolation) and changes in both mean state and variability.

Individualistic species dynamics and the formation of no-analog communities can be explained by a niche-based conceptual framework (Jackson and Overpeck 2000; Figure 1). Each species has a unique, multidimensional fundamental niche. Not all combinations of environmental variables are realized, however, and so portions of a fundamental niche may not exist today. If climate change leads to new combinations of climate variables, species can expand into previously unrealized portions of their fundamental niche. As climate changes, shifts in the realized environmental space may drive disaggregation of some species associations and emergence of others. The formation of no-analog communities, past or future, should therefore result from the development of climates also lacking any modern counterpart.

In this conceptualization, species' niches are static, but the environment is not. The model is best suited to time scales at which rates of environmental change are large relative to rates of evolutionary change. Because rates of evolutionary change are controlled in part by generation time and within-species genetic correlations among traits, the importance of adaptive responses to 21st-century climate change will vary among taxa and will presumably be greatest for organisms with comparatively short (< 1 year) generation times.

## No-analog communities of the past: a late-glacial case study

No-analog fossil assemblages are pervasive in Quaternary paleoecological records, documented for plants, mammals, coleopterans, mollusks, and foraminifera. They occur in terrestrial and marine settings and from high to low latitudes. The best-known cases are from the most recent glacial–interglacial transition in North America.

Networks of fossil pollen data collected from lake and mire sediments reveal the distribution and composition of late-glacial no-analog plant communities in North America. These communities are characterized by anomalously high abundances of some taxa (e.g., Betula), co-occurrences at high abundances of taxa now nearly allopatric (e.g., Picea, Fraxinus), and/or very low abundances of now common taxa (e.g., Pinus). No-analog plant associations in Alaska and eastern North America are broadly contemporaneous with each other, and are also apparently contemporaneous with no-analog plant assemblages in Europe and the southwestern US, and with North American no-analog mammalian communities.

Several lines of evidence indicate that development of late-glacial, no-analog plant communities was linked to the occurrence of no-analog climates, characterized by higher-than-present temperature seasonality. Simulations from two climate models indicate that both Alaska and eastern North America had anomalously large annual ranges of insolation and temperature, with the largest temperature ranges coinciding with peak vegetation dissimilarity.

In eastern North America, the high pollen abundances of temperate tree taxa (Fraxinus, Ostrya/Carpinus, Ulmus) in these highly seasonal climates may be explained by their position at the edge of the current North American climate envelope. This suggests that the fundamental niches for these taxa extend beyond the set of climates observed at present.

However, the evidence for higher-than-present seasonality does not rule out effects of other environmental factors (lowered CO2 concentrations, absent or sparse human populations, existence of now-extinct mega-herbivores) upon late-glacial vegetation composition and structure. Lowered CO2 concentrations, in particular, probably affected late-glacial vegetation profoundly, by limiting the amount of carbon substrate for photosynthesis and increasing sensitivity to moisture stress.

## Risk of novel climates by 2100 AD

Risk maps (Figure 4) are based upon analyses of an ensemble of global climate simulations performed for the IPCC 2007 report, from which they calculated the dissimilarity between mean 1980–1999 and 2080–2099 climates across all terrestrial grid-cells (mean grid-cell size was 2.8 degrees latitude by 2.8 degrees longitude). Climate change is represented by a dissimilarity index that integrates four variables: mean summer temperature, winter temperature, summer precipitation, and winter precipitation. A 21st-century climate grid-cell is novel if its combination of seasonal temperature and precipitation differs substantially from all late 20th-century climates.

More formally, a 21st-century climate grid-cell is deemed novel if its dissimilarity to its closest 20th-century match exceeds a critical threshold, defined as the dissimilarity value that optimally discriminates whether a pair of 1980–1999 climate grid-cells were drawn from the same or different biomes.

The analysis uses two IPCC scenarios: A2 (atmospheric pCO2 reaches 850 ppm by 2100 AD, not yet stabilized) and B1 (pCO2 stabilized at 550 ppm by 2100 AD).

Projected novel climates are concentrated in tropical and sub-tropical regions. This spatial fingerprint is the result of global warming: as the world warms, the warmest areas are the first to move outside the present climate space. Precipitation changes are important, but secondary. In the A2 simulations, novel climates are likely to develop in lowland Amazonia, the southeastern US, the African Sahara and Sahel, the eastern Arabian Peninsula, southeast India and China, the IndoPacific, and northern Australia. Temperate and upper latitudes have little apparent risk of future novel climates, at least by 2100.

When the pool of potential 20th-century analogs is constrained to grid-cells within 500 km (an extreme upper-end estimate of unassisted plant migration capabilities by 2100 AD), regionally novel climates are globally pervasive in the A2 simulations.

## Implications for ecological forecasting

Novel climates represent a serious challenge for forecasting ecological responses to climate change. Bioclimatic niche models are widely used to predict future species range shifts and extinction risks, but are based on correlations between current climates and species distributions. Even in the absence of biotic limitations, fundamental niches will be incompletely represented by niche models if the modeled niches are not fully circumscribed by current climates.

The no-analog problem only affects niche modeling when (1) the envelope of observed climates truncates a fundamental niche and (2) the direction of environmental change causes currently unobserved portions of a species' fundamental niche to open up (Figure 5).

Species-level uncertainties accumulate at the community level owing to ecological interactions, so the composition and structure of communities in novel climate regimes will be difficult to predict. Increases in atmospheric CO2 should increase the temperature optimum for photosynthesis and reduce sensitivity to moisture stress, weakening the foundation for applying present empirical plant–climate relationships.

Mechanistic ecological models (e.g., dynamic global vegetation models) are in principle better suited for predicting responses to novel climates. However, in practice, most include only a limited number of plant functional types or are partially parameterized using modern ecological observations.

Past no-analog climates differ from those we will encounter in the future, but they can be used to test the robustness of ecological models. Demonstrating that ecological models can accurately simulate past species distributions and community composition is necessary but not sufficient to impart confidence in future predictions.

The challenge is compounded by the concentration of future novel climates in regions of high ecological complexity and diversity. Human land use, landscape fragmentation, biological invasions, increases in atmospheric CO2, and other biogeochemical shifts will interact with novel climates to yield yet more ecological surprises.

## Conclusions: "Here there be dragons"

Novel climates represent uncharted portions of climate space, where we have no observational data to parameterize and validate ecological forecasts. Ecological forecasts for novel climates will always be less certain because they are inherently extrapolative. Given the risk of ecological surprises and the loss of ecological services in a greenhouse world, there is considerable societal value in keeping climate "on the map", within the range of optimal predictive capacity.

## What is a "no-analog" community? (Panel 1)

No-analog communities consist of species that are extant today, but in combinations not found at present. "No-analog" is therefore shorthand for "no present analog" and can refer to both past and potential future communities. This definition casts no-analog communities as ecological, not evolutionary, phenomena, because it is assumed that the constituent species still exist today but are reshuffled into combinations not found at present. The authors use "no-analog" and "novel" interchangeably.
