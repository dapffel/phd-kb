# Accelerating extinction risk from climate change

**Author:** Mark C. Urban
**Journal:** Science (2015) 348(6234):571–573
**DOI:** 10.1126/science.aaa4984
**Type:** Meta-analysis

---

## Summary

Meta-analysis of 131 published multispecies predictions of extinction risk from climate change. Estimated a global mean extinction rate using Bayesian Markov chain Monte Carlo random-effects meta-analysis, with each study weighted by sample size. Evaluated how extinction risk varied depending on future global temperature increases, taxonomic groups, geographic regions, endemism, modelling techniques, dispersal assumptions, and extinction thresholds.

## Overall result

7.9% of species predicted to become extinct from climate change (95% CIs: 6.2–9.8%). Result robust to model type, weighting scheme, statistical method, potential publication bias, and missing studies.

## Extinction risk accelerates with temperature

Future global extinction risk predicted not only to increase but to accelerate as global temperatures rise (regression coefficient = 0.53; CIs: 0.46–0.61):
- Current (0.8°C post-industrial): 2.8%
- Policy target (2°C): 5.2%
- RCP 6.0 (3°C): 8.5%
- RCP 8.5 (4.3°C): 15.7% — threatens one in six species

## Geographic variation

Regions differed significantly in extinction risk (ΔDIC = 12.6):
- North America: 5%
- Europe: 6%
- Australia and New Zealand: 14%
- South America: 23%

South America, Australia, and New Zealand face no-analog climates, harbor diverse assemblages of endemic species with small ranges. Australia and New Zealand further limited by small land masses restricting habitat shifts. Most predictions (60%) center on North America and Europe — need to refocus toward less studied and more threatened regions. Only four studies in Asia.

## Endemism

Endemic species face a 6% greater extinction risk relative to models including both endemic and non-endemic species (ΔDIC = 8.3). Extinction risks also rose faster with temperature for models with endemic species (ΔDIC = 8.2).

## Taxonomic groups

Extinction risks did NOT vary significantly by taxonomic group (ΔDIC = 0.7), contrary to predictions. Possible explanations: trait variation at finer taxonomic scales may be more important; typical approaches may not capture the full range of differences among groups.

## Extinction thresholds

Studies differed in how much habitat loss commits a species to extinction (100%, 95%, or 80% thresholds). Extinction thresholds were second only to expected climate change in explaining variation. Decreasing threshold from 100% (no extinction debt) to 80% increased risk from 5% to 15% (ΔDIC = 144.1). Lower thresholds also increased the rise in extinction risk with future temperatures (interaction ΔDIC = 5.9).

## Dispersal assumptions

Dispersal assumptions significantly affected risks (ΔDIC = 68.5):
- Universal dispersal: 6%
- Species-specific dispersal: 10%
- No dispersal: 12%

Species-specific dispersal resulted in risks midway between no- and universal-dispersal assumptions.

## Modelling technique

Only marginal effect of modelling technique on extinction risk (ΔDIC = 3.4):
- Species distribution models: 7%
- Mechanistic models: 8%
- Expert opinion: 18%
- Species-area relationships: 22%

Species-area models explicitly incorporate extinction debt and can overestimate due to a sampling artefact. High expert-opinion risks could stem from broader biological understanding, more pessimistic outlook, or greater uncertainty.

## Important caveats

- Majority of studies extrapolate correlations between current climate and species distributions to novel conditions
- Omit important biological mechanisms: species interactions, evolution, landscape dispersal barriers, habitat degradation, intraspecific trait variation
- Depending on the mechanism, consideration can either increase or decrease predicted risks
- Evolution can decrease risks by allowing adaptation; anthropogenic barriers can increase risks by limiting dispersal
- Extinction risks are likely much smaller than total number of species influenced by climate change — even non-threatened species could experience substantial changes in abundances, distributions, and interactions

## Study composition

- Majority used correlations between current distributions and climate (SDMs/climate envelope models)
- Smaller number used process-based models of physiology or demography (15%), species-area relationships (5%), or expert opinion (4%)
